## The Pre-Alphabetic World

### Writing Before Alphabets

Before the Phoenician revolution, the ancient world used several writing systems:

**Cuneiform (Mesopotamia)**
- Developed around 3400 BCE in Sumer
- Used wedge-shaped marks pressed into clay
- Originally pictographic, evolved to represent syllables
- Required learning 600-1000 signs

**Egyptian Hieroglyphs**
- Developed around 3200 BCE
- Combined logographic and alphabetic elements
- Required learning several hundred signs
- Mostly limited to priestly and scribal classes

**Chinese Characters**
- Developed around 1200 BCE (possibly earlier)
- Each character represents a word or morpheme
- Requires learning thousands of characters
- Still in use today

### The Literacy Problem

These complex writing systems created a fundamental barrier: literacy was restricted to specialists who spent years mastering hundreds or thousands of signs. Writing was a technology of the eliteâ€”priests, scribes, and administrators.

The Phoenicians would change this forever.

### What Made It Revolutionary

1. **Simplicity**: Only 22 symbols to learn

### What About Chinese, Japanese, Korean?

Chinese characters developed independently and are not descended from Phoenician.

However:
- **Japanese** uses Chinese characters (kanji) alongside syllabic scripts (hiragana, katakana)
- **Korean Hangul** was deliberately designed in the 15th century as an original system
- Both languages heavily use loanwords written in adapted scripts

## RTL and LTR: Why Do Writing Directions Differ?

### The Phoenician Direction

Phoenician was written **right-to-left (RTL)**, likely because:
- Most people are right-handed
- Carving on stone from right to left prevents the hand from covering fresh work
- Earlier Semitic scripts used RTL

### The Greek Switch

Early Greek used **boustrophedon** writingâ€”alternating directions line by line (like an ox plowing a field). Eventually, Greeks standardized on **left-to-right (LTR)**, possibly because:
- Writing with ink (rather than carving) favors LTR for right-handed writers
- The writing hand doesn't smear fresh ink

### The Split Inheritance

This directional split explains today's divide:
- **RTL scripts**: Arabic, Hebrew, Syriac (Aramaic heritage)
- **LTR scripts**: Latin, Greek, Cyrillic (Greek heritage)

Both directions trace back to the same Phoenician sourceâ€”a fascinating example of how writing systems evolve differently in different cultural contexts.
